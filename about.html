How It All Began

My story begins long before I was born, somewhere in the 1960-s in Soviet Ukraine, where my grandparents were building rocket ships.

My grandfather was an engineer and my grandmother was a programmer for a large air-space engineering burro. One of grandmother’s projects was awarded at an Exhibitions of Achievements of National Economy for the whole Soviet Union, which was a huge achievement.

Grandparents raised me with a notion that there’s nothing more powerful than science. Nevertheless, I followed my mother’s steps, got master’s degree in corporate finance, and built a strong career in investment banking in Ukraine.

Something about classic financial markets and overall ethics of the industry was keeping me dissatisfied. Being passionate about changing the world for good pushed me in all sorts of projects connected to sustainability, longevity, or social responsibility. I had a gut feeling that I need better instruments to be able to build something that can provoke change.

When I moved to California three years ago, following the love of my life, and trying to reestablish myself on a new “planet”, I realized that I have a blank canvas and a capable brain to perceive what I wanted for so long. My grandmother’s genes started to show and I pursued a new career of a full stack software engineer.

The Future's So Bright, I Gotta Wear Shades

In August 2021 I signed up for the MIT xPro Professional Certificate: Full Stack Development with MERN, a 9-month long program, covering web development, front-end development with react, and back-end development with MongoDB and Node.js.

To be able to progress faster and make sure I am not missing out on anything, I supplement MIT program with different open source courses including Codecademy pro, Harvard's CS50, reading all the books I can find on relevant subjects and practicing daily with Codewars and Frontend Mentor.

I am building a habit of applying the best practices in coding for each of my projects, such as writing unit testing for more complex assignments, and simply using GitHub pull requests and doing descriptive commits on each iteration.
